# Consumer Psychology Foundations

## When to Use This Skill

- Writing marketing copy, landing pages, or CTAs
- Designing onboarding flows or activation sequences
- Creating retention or re-engagement mechanics
- Crafting social proof or testimonial strategies
- Understanding why users don't convert (diagnosis)
- Building habit-forming product features

---

## The Collector's Loop

Many digital products target people who save/bookmark/hoard content but rarely consume it.

```
DISCOVER → SAVE → GUILT → (repeat)
```

### The Psychology

1. **Saving feels productive** — The act of bookmarking triggers a small dopamine hit. "I'll get to this later" feels like progress.

2. **Backlog creates anxiety** — As saved items pile up, they become a reminder of unfulfilled intentions.

3. **Anxiety makes consumption harder** — The larger the backlog, the more overwhelming it feels to start.

4. **So they save more instead** — Saving new things is easier than tackling the backlog. The loop continues.

### Breaking the Loop

Products that help collectors must offer:
- **Immediate consumption** — Not "save for later," but "consume now"
- **Guilt reduction** — "You don't have to read everything"
- **Progress feeling** — Completion metrics, streaks, "you finished X"
- **Permission** — "It's okay to skip things"

### Collector Personas

| Type | Behavior | Motivation |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Aspirational Collector** | Saves to become a better version of themselves | Identity/growth |
| **FOMO Collector** | Saves so they don't miss out | Fear/anxiety |
| **Social Collector** | Saves to share or discuss with others | Status/belonging |
| **Reference Collector** | Saves for "just in case" future use | Security/control |

## FOMO Mechanics

Fear Of Missing Out is a powerful motivator, but must be used ethically.

### Types of FOMO

| Type | What They Fear | Trigger Phrases |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Content FOMO** | Missing important ideas or information | "Everyone's talking about...", "The article that changed..." |
| **Social FOMO** | Being left out of conversations | "Join 10,000 others...", "Your peers are already..." |
| **Capability FOMO** | Others having skills/abilities they lack | "While you're still doing X, they're doing Y" |
| **Time FOMO** | Falling behind, missing windows | "Limited time", "Before it's too late" |
| **Status FOMO** | Missing opportunities for advancement | "Early access", "Exclusive", "Invite-only" |

### Ethical Use

**Do this:**
- Use FOMO to motivate genuine benefit
- Be truthful about scarcity/timing
- Provide real value that justifies urgency

**Don't do this:**
- Manufacture false scarcity
- Exploit anxiety without delivering value
- Create FOMO that leads to regret

### Effective FOMO Triggers

| Trigger | Example | Why It Works |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| Specific numbers | "2,341 people signed up today" | Concrete > vague |
| Implicit comparison | "Most people don't know this" | Creates in-group/out-group |
| Time pressure | "Price increases Friday" | Forces decision |
| Social proof + FOMO | "Your competitors are using this" | Combines two motivators |

## Social Proof Dynamics

People look to others' behavior to guide their own decisions.

### Social Proof Ladder (Most → Least Effective)

1. **Peer behavior** — "People like you are doing X"
2. **Quantified behavior** — "2,341 people did X today"
3. **Authority endorsement** — "Recommended by [Expert]"
4. **Platform endorsement** — "Featured in [Publication]"
5. **Generic claims** — "Trusted by thousands"

### Social Proof Flywheel

```
User takes visible action
         │
         ▼
Their network sees it
         │
         ▼
Some percentage converts
         │
         ▼
They take visible action
         │
         └──────→ (repeat)
```

### Making Actions Visible

Products that grow virally make user actions visible:
- Public by default (with opt-out)
- Easy sharing mechanics
- Status indicators (badges, streaks)
- Social feeds of activity

### Proof Placement

| Location | Best For |
|----------|----------|
| Above the fold | Reducing bounce, building initial trust |
| Near CTA | Overcoming final hesitation |
| During onboarding | Reducing abandonment |
| In-product | Encouraging deeper engagement |

## Identity-Based Marketing

People make decisions to reinforce who they believe they are (or want to become).

### The Framework

1. **Mirror their current identity** — Show you understand who they are now
2. **Offer an aspirational identity** — Show who they could become
3. **Position product as the bridge** — Your product enables the transformation

### Example Patterns

| Current Identity | Aspirational Identity | Bridge |
|------------------|----------------------|--------|
| "I'm always behind on reading" | "I'm someone who stays informed" | [Your product] |
| "I'm overwhelmed by email" | "I have inbox zero" | [Your product] |
| "I'm not technical" | "I can build things" | [Your product] |

### Copy Patterns

**Mirror + Aspiration:**
- "For the 'I'll do it later' crowd: later is now."
- "Stop being the person who bookmarks. Be the person who reads."
- "You're not bad with money. You just need better tools."

**Identity Verbs:**
- "Join" (belonging)
- "Become" (transformation)
- "Unlock" (potential)
- "Master" (competence)

## Decision-Making Moments

Users don't exist in a vacuum. Understanding their context improves conversion.

### The Decision Context

Before designing any touchpoint, ask:

1. **Where are they coming from?** (Ad? Referral? Search? Existing content?)
2. **What state are they in?** (Active research? Passive browsing? Urgent need?)
3. **What are their alternatives?** (Do nothing? Competitor? DIY?)
4. **What's the friction?** (Time? Money? Trust? Complexity?)

### The Four Options Framework

At any decision point, users mentally weigh options:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    USER'S OPTIONS                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  1. Do the hard thing (high effort, uncertain reward)   │
│  2. Do nothing (low effort, guilt/FOMO)                 │
│  3. Find an alternative (effort to research)            │
│  4. Use your product (new option you're offering)       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Your job**: Make Option 4 obviously better than 1-3.

### Reducing Decision Friction

| Friction Type | Solution |
|---------------|----------|
| **Cognitive load** | Simplify choices, use defaults |
| **Trust deficit** | Social proof, guarantees, transparency |
| **Effort perception** | Show ease, quick wins, progress |
| **Risk aversion** | Free trials, money-back, low commitment |
| **Status quo bias** | Show cost of inaction |

## Trust Sequences

Trust is built progressively, not all at once.

### The Trust Ladder

```
STRANGER → CURIOUS → ENGAGED → TRUSTING → CONVERTED → ADVOCATE
```

Each step requires a small "yes" before asking for a bigger one.

### Micro-Commitments

| Stage | Ask For | Give In Return |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Curious | Attention (3 seconds) | Hook, intrigue |
| Engaged | Time (30 seconds) | Value, insight |
| Trusting | Information (email) | More value, access |
| Converted | Money/commitment | Full product |
| Advocate | Referral | Status, rewards |

### The 3-Second Rule

You have ~3 seconds to earn the next 30 seconds. In those 3 seconds:
- Confirm they're in the right place
- Promise value
- Reduce perceived effort

### Trust Signals by Stage

| Stage | Effective Signals |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Stranger** | Professional design, clear value prop, recognizable logos |
| **Curious** | Specificity, social proof numbers, clear benefit |
| **Engaged** | Testimonials, case studies, demonstration |
| **Trusting** | Guarantees, security badges, transparent pricing |

## Cognitive Biases Reference

### Biases That Drive Action

| Bias | Definition | Application |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **Completion bias** | Desire to finish what's started | Progress bars, "almost there" |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Valuing past investment | "You've already done X, don't stop now" |
| **Loss aversion** | Losses feel 2x worse than equivalent gains | Frame as "don't miss" vs "get access" |
| **Social proof** | Following others' behavior | Numbers, testimonials, "popular" labels |
| **Authority bias** | Trusting experts | Expert endorsements, credentials |
| **Scarcity** | Valuing rare things more | Limited time, limited spots |
| **Reciprocity** | Returning favors | Give value before asking |
| **Commitment consistency** | Acting consistent with past behavior | Small yeses before big asks |

### Biases That Block Action

| Bias | Definition | How to Overcome |
|------|------------|-----------------|
| **Status quo bias** | Preference for current state | Show cost of inaction |
| **Analysis paralysis** | Too many options freeze decisions | Reduce choices, recommend defaults |
| **Ambiguity aversion** | Avoiding unclear outcomes | Be specific about what happens next |
| **Present bias** | Overvaluing immediate vs future | Immediate rewards, instant gratification |

## Applying These Models

### For Landing Pages
1. Identify the decision context (where are they coming from?)
2. Mirror their current identity/pain
3. Show the aspirational identity/outcome
4. Use appropriate social proof for their stage
5. Reduce friction to first micro-commitment

### For Onboarding
1. Start with quick wins (completion bias)
2. Build small commitments (consistency)
3. Show progress (completion bias)
4. Introduce social proof at friction points
5. Celebrate milestones (identity reinforcement)

### For Retention
1. Break the collector's loop (if applicable)
2. Create visible habits/streaks
3. Enable social sharing (flywheel)
4. Reinforce identity transformation
5. Use loss aversion for churn prevention

### For Re-engagement
1. Acknowledge the gap without guilt
2. Lower the bar to return
3. Show what they're missing (FOMO)
4. Remind of past investment (sunk cost)
5. Offer a fresh start option

## Ethical Guardrails

These models are powerful. Use them responsibly:

1. **Deliver real value** — Persuasion without value is manipulation
2. **Be truthful** — Don't manufacture false scarcity or fake social proof
3. **Respect autonomy** — Make it easy to opt out, unsubscribe, cancel
4. **Avoid exploitation** — Don't target vulnerable populations or exploit anxieties
5. **Consider long-term** — Short-term manipulation destroys long-term trust

**The test**: Would you be comfortable if your tactics were fully transparent to the user?
